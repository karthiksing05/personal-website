Yelling Through
the Whispers

What India's Largest Monuments Reveal About India's Hidden Agenda

By Karthik Singaravadivelan
Shaurya Smarak · Bhopal Telangana Martyrs Memorial · Hyderabad

India is well-known for the constant entanglement of religion and politics, and its monument scene continues to embody this dichotomy. With temples, statues, and a rich history of fighting for religious freedom and democratic ideals, India's monuments tell a brave story of war and struggle in the face of oppression.

Through a legacy shaped by diverse linguistic groups belonging to Indo-Aryan, Dravidian, Austroasiatic, and Sino-Tibetan language families, and as the birthplace of four major world religions—Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, and Sikhism—India tells a strong story of diversity and strength. According to the People's Linguistic Survey of India, the country is home to 780 languages, though only 22 are officially recognized under the Eighth Schedule of the Indian Constitution.

The Shaurya Smarak

The Shaurya Smarak is "everything a good Indian monument should be." When I first saw this monument in person, I was only 9 years old, and was taken aback by the dichotomy of the serenity of the garden, and the gloom of the helmets of fallen soldiers on posts. This was a monument that encapsulated duty, and as I delved into news articles and my own memory, these feelings were only reinforced.

Inaugurated on October 14, 2016, by Prime Minister Narendra Modi, the Shaurya Smarak is centered around the Shaurya Stambh, a 62-foot tall granite pillar with various colors intertwining to represent the three branches of the Indian military. The memorial spans 12 acres in the heart of Arera Hills, Bhopal. Around the Stambh, the names of martyred soldiers are inscribed on glass boards.

The Shaurya Smarak is a testimony to a collection of battles—namely the Indo-Pak War of 1947–48, the Indo-Pak War of 1965, the Indo-Pak War of 1971 which led to the creation of Bangladesh, and the Kargil War of 1999. One of its most compelling features recreates the freezing conditions of the Siachen Glacier, offering visitors a first-hand understanding of what soldiers endure in those war zones.

The Telangana Martyrs Memorial

If the Shaurya Smarak is a testament to the reality of war, the Telangana Martyrs Memorial is a sermon of the glory of sacrifice. Setting multiple world records, it is the largest seamless stainless-steel structure ever built—five times larger than Chicago's Cloud Gate—and spans over 85,000 square feet on the banks of Hussain Sagar lake in Hyderabad.

The memorial's centerpiece is an earthen lamp (diya) over 80 feet tall, designed by sculptor M.V. Ramana Reddy. The diya is a symbol of the triumph of good over evil, integral to Hindu festivals like Diwali—a flame that burns perpetually at 45 meters above ground level. Over 5,000 laborers, 100 engineers, and 500 technicians built the structure, which took four years to complete.

The Telangana Martyrs Memorial was inaugurated in June 2023 to commemorate the 1969 Agitation, one of the largest civilian uprisings in Indian history. Approximately 369 people died during this movement—most of them students, killed in police firing—as they demanded Telangana be recognized as a separate state. The 1969 Agitation was a conflict not between nations, but between the national government and its own people.

The Common Language of Monuments

From looking at these two monuments, one can only infer that India reveres these two subjects similarly. Both monuments centralize around a spire, the conical structure echoing famous religious ideals of the cyclic nature of life and death in Hinduism. To Indians, sacrifice is returning to the motherland in your most primordial form—a reflection of all Indians' strong ties to reincarnation. Both also feature educational museums honoring those who've received medals of honor for their service, as well as informational exhibits to educate citizens on the sacrifices that have occurred.

What Makes This Monument Different

The Telangana Martyrs Memorial has only one difference from the Shaurya Smarak, and every other monument that most of India would consider typical—it's made to honor students. Almost no monuments in India are made to revere intra-Indian conflict. The Shaurya Smarak is one of countless monuments built to honor Indian soldier sacrifice on foreign land, but the Telangana Martyrs Memorial is one of the only monuments to celebrate a conflict within India. Additionally, the memorial honors civilians who died at the hands of their own government's police forces during peaceful protests—a radical departure from traditional military commemoration.

But that's not all. The current iteration of the memorial is actually the second iteration. There was a Gun Park Memorial before it, designed by sculptor Aekka Yadagiri Rao and enacted in 1975. A humble 25 feet high, this first memorial was originally built through grassroots crowdfunding and stood for nearly half a century before being embellished with the world's largest seamless steel structure—a monument built not by government directive, but by popular demand that the government could no longer ignore.

Conclusion

The implications are clear. India is so quick to build monuments that personify a story of camaraderie, a public-facing angle that unites the nation. But even amongst neighborhoods, there are divides. India's diversity of language—with over 780 languages and a religious composition of 79.8% Hindu, 14.2% Muslim, 2.3% Christian, and 1.7% Sikh—contributes to a constant shift and growth of India's ideals.

The wait from grassroots memorial (1975) to government-funded monument (2023) took nearly fifty years—half a century during which the martyrs' sacrifice was acknowledged locally but not nationally. This delay speaks volumes about which sacrifices India's government chooses to monumentalize quickly, and which it reluctantly honors only after persistent public pressure. The Shaurya Smarak Foundation was established in 2010 and the monument built by 2016. Six years. For the martyrs of 1969: forty-eight.

These monuments should not be ignored. Conflict is an important part of history, even if it's between individuals "on the same team." India will continue to be one of the most diverse nations on this planet, with over 780 languages, 22 officially recognized languages under the Eighth Schedule of the Indian Constitution, and six religions awarded national minority status. The Telangana Martyrs Memorial stands as proof that the voices yelling through the whispers—the grassroots movements, the student protesters, the civilians demanding autonomy—eventually become too loud to ignore. And when that happens, even the government must listen.
